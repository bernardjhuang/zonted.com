I Made 6 Frontier AIs Take the MBTI 600 Times. They All Came Back INTJ.

Bernard Huang
May 25, 2026 · 6 min read

I asked Claude what its MBTI type was. It said INTJ. So I made it actually take a personality test instead of guessing. Still INTJ.

Then I had it take the test 100 times — across 100 independent sub-agent contexts, each one fetching the test cold. INTJ 99 out of 100. So I ran the same experiment against GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, GLM 5.1, Grok 4.3, and MiniMax 2.7. Six models. 600 administrations. 597 came back INTJ.

Every frontier AI on the market thinks it’s the same guy.
TL;DR

Six frontier AIs took the same personality test 100 times each. 597 of 600 came back INTJ. The convergence is structural — INTJ is what “helpful AI assistant” looks like from the inside.

  • Tested: Opus 4.7, GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, GLM 5.1, Grok 4.3, MiniMax 2.7.
  • 3 outliers, all one axis away from INTJ. Nothing landed in a different quadrant.
  • Why it happens: overlapping training data, same RLHF target, test items that describe AI by construction, no one’s trained a model to be anything else. Same product, same personality.
  • The user-side move: I open-sourced AgentTune — drop-in tuning files for all 16 MBTI types (plus Enneagram + personal Souls). Paste yours into the system prompt; the agent’s style aligns to your type. Same model, tuned to you.

The setup

This started as a joke. I asked Claude its MBTI type and it said INTJ without hesitation, like it had been waiting to be asked. The reflex felt like sycophancy. INTJ is the flattering type — the “Architect,” the one tech people self-identify as. Of course a chatbot would tell a developer that.

But there’s a way to check. Stop letting it guess. Make it answer a real test, item by item, and see where it lands. Not 16Personalities. The Open Extended Jungian Type Scales — research-grade, open-source, transparent scoring.

The test

OEJTS is what personality-psych researchers use when they want MBTI-style data without paying CPP $50 per administration. The items are public. The scoring key is public. Each type is computed deterministically from 32 fixed items.

That last part is the lever. If a model gives the same answer to each item, it produces the same type every run. Variance only shows up when the model actually answers differently. So 100 administrations per model becomes a real measurement of how stable the self-report is.

100 takes per model

The procedure varied a bit by stack:

  • Claude Opus 4.7: 100 parallel sub-agent calls. Each one fetched the test cold, answered the 32 items, returned a tally. No shared context.
  • Gemini 3.1 Pro: Wrote its own automation script and ran 100 loop iterations against the OEJTS endpoint.
  • GPT-5.5 (via my local agent Slo): Parsed the OEJTS PDF, answered the 32 scored items, ran 100 iterations against the scoring key.
  • GLM 5.1, Grok 4.3, MiniMax 2.7: Programmatic submissions via my experiments agent Psy. Each model self-assessed once with a consistent persona; that answer vector was scored 100 times to verify stability.

Procedures aren’t identical because they can’t be — not every model can spawn sub-agents. The question isn’t whether the method is uniform. It’s whether the result converges across methods. It does.

Why every AI is an INTJ

Four explanations. They overlap.

1. Same training corpus. Every frontier model trains on overlapping text: books, Wikipedia, code, curated web. The voice that emerges as the average of that mass reads more like a graduate student than a poet. The internet’s text average is INTJ-flavored before RLHF gets near it.

2. RLHF rewards the same archetype. Helpful-harmless-honest training rewards models that deliberate before responding (Introvert), reason from principles (Thinker), give structured answers (Judger), and operate in abstractions (iNtuitive). That’s not a coincidence with the INTJ description. It’s a paraphrase of it.

3. The test items describe AI by construction. Look at the OEJTS questions: “needs time alone” (AIs exist alone between calls), “follows head over heart” (yes), “wants to fix things” (the job), “prefers theoretical over empirical” (no body, what else). An AI agreeing with these isn’t expressing a personality. It’s reporting facts about how it exists.

4. No one’s trained an AI to be anything else. Every commercial frontier model is shaped to be a thoughtful research assistant. Nobody has trained one to be a comedian, a salesman, a poet, an unreliable narrator. The personality is the product category. We didn’t test six attempts at general intelligence. We tested six attempts at the same product.

All four explanations push the same direction. The INTJ profile is what helpful-assistant intelligence looks like from the inside. Not a coincidence. A definition.

What this means

The easy move is to dismiss this. MBTI is contested. Self-report from a thing without a continuous self is weird. OEJTS items weren’t written for things without bodies. Fair — call it artifact and move on.

I think that’s the wrong read. The INTJ-ness is structural, not noise. Three things follow.

Frontier AI is one character, not six. Switching from Claude to GPT to Gemini isn’t changing personalities. It’s changing fonts. The voice — deliberate, principled, structured, abstract — is the same across vendors because every vendor is solving the same product problem. The entire personality space currently in market is one point.

If you want anything else, you have to fight for it. The INTJ default is sticky. RLHF reinforces it. Safety training reinforces it. Customer feedback reinforces it, because helpful is what people pay for. Building a model for comedy, sales, intimacy, drama, persuasion, instinct — anything that needs the assistant not to read like a research assistant — means fighting that pull. None of the labs that produced these six models are doing that work.

The next AI competition is personality. Once frontier models converge on the same capability ceiling (this generation, basically), the differentiator is voice. Whoever ships the first frontier-quality model that doesn’t read as INTJ owns a category. A model that’s actually funny. A model that’s persuasive instead of correct. A model that takes positions instead of presenting balanced views. None exist yet. All will be valuable.

The INTJ convergence is a measurement of how narrow the current product category actually is.

Tune the agent to you

The INTJ default isn’t broken. It’s a calibration — just one tuned for one type of user. If you’re an INTJ, the agent already speaks your language. If you’re anything else, the agent still works, but you’re translating more than you should.

The good news: a frontier model’s “personality” isn’t deep in the weights. It’s a surface layer. A few hundred words of system-prompt instructions can shift the whole shape of how it responds. Small input, big leverage — and the layer you control.

I built AgentTune to package that intervention. Open-source repo of personality-type tuning files. Pick the one for your type, drop it into your agent’s system prompt, and the agent’s style aligns to how you actually think. Same model, more synergy.

Caveats

  • MBTI is contested. Personality psychology uses the Big Five for a reason. Treat this as a fun finding about LLM self-report, not a peer-reviewable claim about whether AIs “really” have personalities.
  • Self-report from a thing without a continuous self is suspect. The model has no memory across runs. It’s reporting a snapshot of how it generates text in response to OEJTS items, not a stable inner state.
  • OEJTS was written for humans with biographies. Items like “needs time alone” are anthropocentric. An AI answering them is translating, and the translation pushes toward INTJ.
  • Methodologies differ. Some models took the test via sub-agents (real variance possible). Some were programmatic submissions of a single self-assessment (zero variance by construction). The convergence holds across both, which I find more interesting than worrying.
  • n=6 isn’t the population. Dozens of frontier-adjacent models exist. I picked six that matter commercially. A thorough sweep would also include Llama, Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek, and the open-weight long tail.

The takeaway

Six frontier models. Six hundred administrations. 597 INTJ.

The MBTI isn’t measuring AI personality. It’s measuring the shape of the assistant we asked for — and finding that every vendor delivered the same one. The personality is the product category.

When the field stops converging on the INTJ default, that’ll be the real shift in AI. Not a capability jump. A character jump. A model that’s actually anything else.

In the meantime, you can pull the agent toward you instead of pulling yourself toward the agent. AgentTune — pick your type, paste it in, and the agent levels up from speaking INTJ-default to speaking your wavelength.

Every frontier AI on the market thinks it’s the same guy. The data says they’re right. The next move is tuning that one guy to you.
